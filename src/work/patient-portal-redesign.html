Patient Portal Redesign

Redesigned the patient-facing portal to reduce support tickets and improve appointment completion rates across three care pathways.

Company

MedTech Corp

Domain

Healthcare

Product Type

B2B

Role

Lead Designer

Timeline

6 months (Jan – Jun 2020)

Team

1 PM, 2 Engineers, 1 Designer

Hero image / product screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

Patients using MedTech Corp's portal struggled with appointment scheduling, prescription refills, and accessing lab results. Support call volume was increasing 12% quarter-over-quarter, and satisfaction scores had been declining for three consecutive quarters.

My Responsibility

Led end-to-end design — from discovery research and problem framing through wireframes, visual design, usability testing, and developer handoff. Managed stakeholder communication with clinical leadership.

Outcome Summary

32% reduction in support tickets. 24% improvement in appointment completion rate. Portal NPS moved from 28 to 52 within 4 months of launch.

Key Impact Areas

Revenue Growth Retention / Engagement Operational Efficiency

Context & Background

Business Context

MedTech Corp provides healthcare management software to mid-size hospitals and clinic networks. Their patient portal was originally built in 2015 and had grown through feature additions without a cohesive redesign. Customer retention was healthy, but new client acquisition was being hurt by poor portal reviews and demo feedback.

User Context

The portal served two user groups: patients (ages 25–75, varying digital literacy) and clinical staff who needed to manage patient communications. Patients reported confusion with navigation, duplicate workflows for similar tasks, and an inability to find lab results without calling the office.

Organizational & Technical Constraints

The engineering team was mid-migration from a legacy backend to a new API layer. Any redesign had to work with both systems during a 3-month overlap period. The clinical compliance team required HIPAA-compliant UI patterns, adding review cycles to every design iteration.

Why This Mattered

Patient portal quality directly influenced contract renewals. Two enterprise clients had specifically flagged the portal during renewal conversations. Solving this wasn't just a UX improvement — it was a retention and revenue problem.

Problem Framing

Original Problem Statement

"How might we reduce the friction patients experience when accessing core portal functions (scheduling, prescriptions, lab results) — so they can self-serve instead of calling the office?"

Key Assumptions

  • Patients wanted to self-serve but couldn't find the right workflows.
  • Most support calls were about tasks that could be handled digitally.
  • A unified navigation model would outperform the current siloed layout.
  • Older patients would need larger touch targets and clearer labeling, but not a separate experience.

What Was Unclear or Risky

  • Whether clinical staff would accept changes to their communication workflows.
  • Whether the legacy API layer would support real-time lab result rendering.
  • How much training/change management hospital admins would require for the new interface.

How Success Was Defined

Primary: 20%+ reduction in support call volume within 3 months. Secondary: measurable improvement in appointment completion rate and patient NPS. Stretch: zero increase in training-related support tickets for clinical staff.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

User Interviews (12) Support Ticket Analysis Heatmap / Session Recording Contextual Inquiry (3 clinics)

Interviewed 12 patients across three clinic networks (mix of ages, digital comfort levels). Analyzed 800+ support tickets from the previous quarter. Reviewed session recordings from Hotjar to identify drop-off patterns. Visited 3 clinics to observe how staff and patients interacted with the portal in context.

Who Was Involved

Myself (research design and synthesis), 1 PM (stakeholder coordination, interview logistics), clinical liaison (compliance review), 2 hospital admin contacts (participant recruiting).

Key Insights

Insight 1: Patients didn't have a navigation problem — they had a trust problem.

Most patients found the right page but didn't trust the information was current. "Is this my latest result or last year's?" was a recurring theme.

Insight 2: 68% of support calls mapped to 3 tasks.

Appointment rescheduling, prescription refill status, and lab result access accounted for the bulk of inbound calls.

Insight 3: Clinical staff workarounds masked real usability issues.

Nurses had built their own cheat sheets to navigate the admin side. The system worked only because of human effort compensating for bad design.

How Insights Influenced Direction

The trust insight shifted our approach from "simplify the navigation" to "make status and recency visible everywhere." The support ticket analysis focused our scope to three core pathways instead of a full-portal overhaul. The clinical staff finding added a secondary design track we hadn't initially planned.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Full portal redesign

Pros: cohesive experience, long-term scalability. Cons: 12+ month timeline, high engineering cost, risk of scope creep.

Rejected — timeline too long for the business urgency.

Option B: Targeted redesign of 3 core pathways Selected

Pros: focused scope, measurable impact, achievable in 6 months. Cons: other portal areas remain inconsistent.

Selected — best balance of impact, speed, and feasibility.

Option C: Band-aid fixes (improved labels, error messages only)

Pros: fast, low engineering effort. Cons: wouldn't address structural navigation issues or trust problems.

Rejected — insufficient to move the needle on support volume.

Trade-offs Evaluated

  • Speed vs. scope: We chose targeted impact over comprehensiveness. The remaining portal sections would be addressed in a future phase.
  • Patient experience vs. clinical staff workflow: We decided to include a clinical staff UX track to prevent creating new problems while solving old ones.
  • New design system vs. existing components: We built 8 new components that could be reused later, rather than overhauling everything upfront.

Constraints That Shaped Decisions

  • HIPAA compliance requirements added 2-week review cycles to each design milestone.
  • Backend migration meant certain data APIs were unstable, limiting real-time features to the new API endpoints only.
  • Two client renewals were tied to visible portal improvements by Q2 end.

Design Execution

User Flows

Mapped the three core pathways end-to-end: appointment scheduling/rescheduling, prescription refill requests, and lab result access. Reduced the average steps-to-completion from 7 to 4 for appointment booking and from 5 to 3 for lab results.

User flow diagram placeholder

Wireframes

Started with low-fidelity wireframes to test information architecture and layout assumptions. Ran internal reviews with the PM and engineering lead to validate technical feasibility before moving to high-fidelity.

Wireframe — scheduling flow
Wireframe — lab results

Interaction Patterns

Introduced a persistent status bar showing last-updated timestamps on every data section — directly addressing the trust insight from research. Added inline confirmation patterns for appointment changes to reduce anxiety and built progressive disclosure for prescription details so patients weren't overwhelmed on first view.

Visual Design

Applied a clean, high-contrast visual language prioritizing readability for older users. Used the existing brand palette but established new typographic hierarchy and spacing standards.

Design System Contributions

Created 8 reusable components (status indicators, data cards, confirmation modals, inline alerts, timestamp badges, progress steppers, action buttons, empty states) that were later adopted by two other product teams.

Validation & Iteration

Testing Methods

Ran two rounds of moderated usability testing with 8 patients per round. Used task-based scenarios focused on the three core pathways. Also ran an unmoderated test with 25 participants via Maze for the final iteration.

Feedback Received

"I can finally see when my results were last updated. That was my biggest frustration before."

— Patient, age 62, Round 1

"The prescription section is much easier, but I almost missed the refill button."

— Patient, age 34, Round 1

What Changed as a Result

  • Increased the size and prominence of the prescription refill CTA based on discoverability issues in Round 1.
  • Added a "quick actions" section to the dashboard after patients repeatedly asked for shortcuts to frequent tasks.
  • Simplified the appointment confirmation flow from 3 steps to 2 after testing showed unnecessary friction in the review step.

What Didn't Work as Expected

The progressive disclosure pattern for prescription details confused some older users who expected all information to be visible at once. We compromised with an "expand all" option that defaulted to collapsed on mobile but expanded on desktop, based on screen real estate.

Business Impact

Both enterprise clients flagged during renewal discussions signed renewed contracts. The portal improvement was cited as a differentiator in two new client acquisition pitches that closed in Q3.

User Impact

Patients reported feeling more in control of their healthcare information. Clinical staff spent less time fielding calls about information that was now self-serve. The "quick actions" dashboard became the most-used entry point within the first month.

Qualitative Feedback

"This is the first time I haven't had to call the office just to check my lab results. Thank you."

— Patient survey response, 2 months post-launch

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have pushed for clinical staff research earlier in the process. We added that track mid-project, which created timeline pressure. Starting with both user groups from day one would have given us a more complete picture and avoided a few rework cycles.

What This Project Taught Me

That "usability" isn't always the core problem. In this case, the portal was technically usable — users just didn't trust the information it presented. This reframed how I approach research: I now look beyond task completion to understand the emotional and informational needs behind user behavior.

How It Shaped My Approach

This was the project that taught me to scope aggressively. By focusing on 3 pathways instead of 20 screens, we shipped faster, measured impact sooner, and built a case for continued investment. I now default to targeted interventions over comprehensive redesigns whenever possible.
