Rolled Back

Checkout Flow Redesign

A checkout simplification initiative that looked promising in testing but decreased conversion in production. Rolled back after two weeks with documented learnings.

Company

RetailX

Domain

Consumer / E-commerce

Product Type

B2C

Role

Lead Designer

Timeline

3 months (Aug – Oct 2021)

Team

1 PM, 2 Engineers, 1 Analyst, 1 Designer

Checkout flow screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

RetailX's checkout had a 34% abandonment rate. Analytics showed users dropping off at the address form and payment steps. The hypothesis was that a simplified, single-page checkout would improve conversion.

My Responsibility

Led the design effort end-to-end. Conducted user research, designed the new checkout flow, ran usability testing, and worked with the analytics team to define the A/B test framework.

Outcome Summary

Usability testing showed strong improvements. But in production A/B test, the new flow decreased conversion by 8%. The redesign was rolled back after two weeks. Post-mortem identified critical gaps between testing and real-world behavior.

Key Impact Areas

❌ Failed / Rolled Back

Context & Background

Business Context

RetailX was a mid-size e-commerce company selling specialty home goods. Revenue growth was stalling, and the product team identified checkout abandonment as the biggest conversion lever. The CFO had set an aggressive target: 5%+ improvement in checkout completion rate by end of Q4.

User Context

Customers skewed 28–55, with average order values of $85. Many were repeat buyers familiar with the existing checkout flow. Mobile traffic was growing (42% of sessions) but mobile conversion lagged desktop significantly.

Constraints

The engineering team had 6 weeks allocated for implementation after design sign-off. The A/B testing infrastructure was new and untested at scale. The existing checkout was tightly coupled to a legacy payment system, limiting what could be changed without backend refactoring.

Why This Mattered

At $85 average order value and 34% abandonment, even a modest conversion improvement would translate to significant revenue. The projected impact of a 5% improvement was $400K+ annually.

Problem Framing

Problem Statement

"How might we reduce checkout abandonment by simplifying the flow — so more users complete their purchase without friction?"

Key Assumptions

  • Fewer steps = fewer drop-offs.
  • A single-page checkout would feel faster and more modern.
  • Users who started checkout had high purchase intent — friction was the problem, not motivation.
  • Mobile users would benefit most from a streamlined layout.

What Was Risky

  • Repeat buyers had learned the existing flow — a new flow might create confusion.
  • A single page could feel overwhelming with all information visible at once.
  • We were optimizing for a funnel metric without fully understanding why users were abandoning.

Success Criteria

Primary: 5%+ improvement in checkout completion rate measured over 2 weeks of A/B testing. Secondary: no increase in customer support contacts related to checkout. Guard rail: no decrease in average order value.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

Funnel Analytics Session Recordings (50) User Interviews (6) Competitor Benchmarking

Analyzed 3 months of checkout funnel data. Watched 50 session recordings of abandoned checkouts. Interviewed 6 recent customers (3 who completed, 3 who abandoned). Benchmarked checkout flows of 5 comparable e-commerce sites.

Key Insights

Insight 1: Address form was the primary drop-off point.

38% of abandonments happened during address entry. Auto-complete wasn't working for many users.

Insight 2: Users expected a progress indicator.

Several interviewees mentioned not knowing how many steps remained. "I thought there were going to be 10 more pages."

Insight 3: Repeat buyers valued familiarity.

Returning customers had memorized the flow. They knew exactly where to click, even if the design was suboptimal. This insight was underweighted in our final design decision.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Fix the address form only

Pros: targeted, low risk. Cons: wouldn't address the broader perception of a long checkout.

Not selected — felt too incremental for the business target.

Option B: Single-page checkout redesign Selected → Failed

Pros: modern, fewer perceived steps, mobile-friendly. Cons: unfamiliar to repeat buyers, higher cognitive load per page.

Selected — we bet on simplification. We were wrong.

Option C: Progressive enhancement of existing flow

Pros: preserve familiarity, fix specific pain points. Cons: not as dramatic a change, harder to measure in A/B test.

Not selected — in hindsight, this was probably the right call.

Trade-offs (In Hindsight)

  • New vs. familiar: We prioritized a "modern" experience over the learned behavior of existing users. This was the core mistake.
  • Mobile-first vs. cross-device: We over-indexed on mobile improvements while 58% of checkout traffic was desktop.
  • Test environment vs. real world: Our usability tests used new users. Production traffic was 65% repeat buyers. The test population didn't match the real audience.

Design Execution

New Flow Design

Consolidated the existing 4-step checkout (Cart Review → Address → Payment → Confirmation) into a single scrollable page with accordion sections. Added inline validation, address auto-complete, and a persistent order summary sidebar.

Before/after flow comparison placeholder

Visual Design

Clean, focused layout with the goal of reducing visual noise. Removed sidebar navigation, promotional banners, and cross-sells from the checkout page. Every element had to justify its presence.

Validation & Iteration

Usability Testing (Pre-Launch)

Ran moderated usability tests with 8 participants. All were recruited externally (not existing RetailX customers). Task completion rate improved from 72% to 91%. Time-to-checkout decreased by 35%. Satisfaction scores were significantly higher. These results gave us confidence to proceed.

A/B Test (Production)

Launched a 50/50 A/B test to all checkout traffic. Within 3 days, the new flow showed a 5% decrease in completion rate. By day 7, the gap had widened to 8%. We let it run for the full two weeks to ensure statistical significance, then made the call to roll back.

What the Data Showed

  • New users actually completed at a higher rate (+4%) — validating the usability test results.
  • Repeat buyers completed at a lower rate (-14%) — the familiar flow disruption hurt them significantly.
  • Mobile conversion was flat. Desktop conversion dropped.
  • Average order value decreased by $6 — users removed items when they saw the full total earlier in the flow.

Business Impact

The two-week test period resulted in an estimated $18K in lost revenue. However, the learnings from this failure directly informed the subsequent (successful) checkout improvements that recovered and exceeded the original target by Q2 of the following year.

⚠ Failure & Rollback

What Failed

The single-page checkout redesign decreased overall checkout completion by 8% and reduced average order value by $6. The core premise — that fewer steps equals fewer drop-offs — turned out to be wrong for this user base.

Why It Failed

  • Testing bias: Usability tests used new users who had no learned behavior. Production traffic was 65% repeat buyers who had memorized the existing flow.
  • Cognitive overload: Showing all checkout sections at once created the opposite of what we intended — instead of feeling simpler, it felt more complex.
  • Price visibility: Surfacing the total earlier caused sticker shock and item removal — the old flow's progressive disclosure of pricing was actually working in the business's favor.

Signals That Led to Rollback

Day 3: -5% completion rate with p-value < 0.05. Day 7: -8% with high confidence. Customer support contacts about "broken checkout" increased 22%. Analytics showed repeat buyers spending 40% more time on the new checkout (negative signal).

What I Learned

  • Usability test participants must match your actual user base — especially regarding familiarity and repeat behavior.
  • "Simpler" is not the same as "fewer steps." Cognitive load depends on how information is chunked, not just how many pages there are.
  • When your product has loyal repeat users, their muscle memory is an asset. Disrupting it has a real cost.
  • Always have rollback criteria defined before launch — we didn't have clear decision thresholds until mid-test.

How It Influenced Future Work

This failure fundamentally changed how I approach redesigns. I now always segment test results by user type (new vs. returning). I prototype with actual customers, not just recruited participants. And I default to progressive enhancement of existing flows rather than full replacements — especially when there's a significant repeat-user base.

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have tested with existing RetailX customers, not just recruited new users. I would have run a smaller, targeted A/B test with only new users first — where the design actually worked — before exposing it to the full audience. And I would have defined kill criteria before launch, not during.

What This Project Taught Me

That data can validate the wrong conclusion if your test conditions don't match reality. Usability testing is necessary but not sufficient. The gap between "works in the lab" and "works in production" is where experience matters — and I gained a lot of that experience from this failure.

How It Shaped My Approach

This is the project I reference most when talking about design maturity. Being willing to ship something, watch it fail, document why, and apply those learnings is more valuable than a portfolio of safe successes. I now treat every launch as a hypothesis, not a solution.
