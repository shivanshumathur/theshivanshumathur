Workflow Automation Dashboard

Designed an internal dashboard that automated manual reporting workflows, saving the operations team 20+ hours per week.

Company

CloudOps Inc.

Domain

SaaS / Internal Tools

Product Type

Internal Platform

Role

Sole Designer

Timeline

4 months (Mar – Jun 2018)

Team

1 PM, 3 Engineers, 1 Designer

Dashboard screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

The operations team at CloudOps spent 20+ hours per week manually compiling reports from 4 different systems. Data was inconsistent, deadlines were frequently missed, and errors in manual data entry led to downstream decisions being made on unreliable information.

My Responsibility

Sole designer on the project. Responsible for user research with the ops team, information architecture, interface design, and iterating based on internal feedback. Collaborated directly with the engineering team on feasibility and data pipeline design.

Outcome Summary

Reduced weekly manual reporting time from 20+ hours to under 2 hours. Eliminated data inconsistency across systems. Dashboard became the single source of truth for executive reporting.

Key Impact Areas

Automation / Cost Reduction Operational Efficiency

Context & Background

Business Context

CloudOps provided cloud infrastructure monitoring and managed services. As the customer base grew from 50 to 200+ accounts, the operations team was drowning in manual reporting. Reports were compiled weekly from Salesforce, Jira, internal monitoring tools, and Google Sheets — each with different data formats and update cadences.

User Context

Primary users were 8 operations managers and 2 executives who consumed weekly reports. The ops managers spent most of Monday and Tuesday pulling data, reconciling mismatches, and formatting reports. The executives needed high-level summaries but often received them late or incomplete.

Constraints

No dedicated data engineering team — the backend work would be handled by the product engineering team alongside their regular sprint work. Budget for external tools was limited, so everything had to be built in-house on top of existing infrastructure.

Why This Mattered

The manual reporting process was a scaling bottleneck. If the company continued to grow, they'd need to hire more ops staff just to maintain reporting — not to improve service quality. This was fundamentally a cost and efficiency problem masquerading as "we need more people."

Problem Framing

Original Problem Statement

"How might we consolidate reporting from multiple systems into a single dashboard — so the ops team can shift from data collection to analysis and action?"

Key Assumptions

  • Most reporting data could be pulled via APIs from existing systems.
  • Ops managers would trust automated data if they could see update timestamps and data sources.
  • A real-time dashboard would be more valuable than scheduled email reports.
  • Executives needed fewer, more focused metrics — not more data.

What Was Risky

  • Data quality from source systems was inconsistent — automation might surface problems that were previously hidden by human judgment.
  • Ops managers might resist a tool that reduced their visible workload (and perceived value).

Success Criteria

Primary: reduce weekly reporting time by 75%+. Secondary: executives receive accurate reports on time every week. Stretch: ops team redirects freed-up time to proactive customer outreach.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

Contextual Observation (2 weeks) Workflow Mapping Stakeholder Interviews (6)

Shadowed the ops team for two full reporting cycles (Mondays and Tuesdays) to understand every manual step. Mapped the end-to-end workflow across all 4 systems. Interviewed all 8 ops managers and both executives to understand what they actually needed from reports versus what they were producing.

Key Insights

Insight 1: 60% of report data was never read by executives.

Ops managers compiled 40+ metrics weekly, but executives consistently only referenced 8-10. The rest existed because "we've always included it."

Insight 2: The reconciliation step was where most errors occurred.

When merging data from different systems, ops managers used heuristics and judgment calls that weren't documented. Different people reconciled differently.

Insight 3: Ops managers wanted to be analysts, not data entry clerks.

Contrary to our fear of resistance, the team was eager to automate. They saw the dashboard as an opportunity to do higher-value work.

How Insights Influenced Direction

The "60% unread" insight let us dramatically simplify the dashboard scope — we focused on the 10 metrics that executives actually used. The reconciliation finding meant we needed to define clear data rules with the engineering team, not just pull raw numbers. And the team's enthusiasm shaped our approach to rollout — they became co-designers rather than just end users.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Buy an off-the-shelf BI tool (Tableau, Looker)

Pros: faster initial setup. Cons: expensive per-seat licensing, limited customization, still required data pipeline work.

Rejected — budget constraints and customization needs.

Option B: Build a custom internal dashboard Selected

Pros: fully customizable, no licensing costs, could be built incrementally. Cons: engineering time, longer build.

Selected — aligned with budget and gave us full control over the experience.

Option C: Automate spreadsheet generation only

Pros: minimal engineering effort, familiar format. Cons: still required manual review, no real-time capability.

Rejected — wouldn't solve the core problem, just speed up a broken process.

Trade-offs

  • Build time vs. licensing cost: We chose to invest 4 months of engineering time to avoid $30K+/year in licensing — a decision that paid off within 6 months.
  • Comprehensive vs. focused: We shipped with 10 core metrics first, with a plan to expand. This meant some ops managers had to keep using spreadsheets for edge cases temporarily.
  • Real-time vs. daily refresh: Engineering capacity limited us to daily data refreshes initially, which was still a massive improvement over weekly manual pulls.

Design Execution

Information Architecture

Organized the dashboard into three views: Executive Summary (high-level KPIs), Operational Detail (filterable data tables), and Trend Analysis (week-over-week comparisons). Each view served a different user need without duplicating data.

IA diagram placeholder

Wireframes

Started with whiteboard sketches with the ops team. Iterated through 3 rounds of wireframes — each reviewed with both ops managers and executives to ensure both audiences were served.

Visual Design

Designed a data-dense but scannable interface. Used card-based layouts for KPIs, color-coded status indicators, and contextual tooltips to explain what each metric meant. Prioritized glanceability — the executive view could be understood in under 30 seconds.

Validation & Iteration

Testing Approach

Ran the dashboard in parallel with the manual process for 3 weeks. Ops managers used both and flagged any data discrepancies. This built trust and uncovered edge cases in the automated data pipeline.

Feedback

"I can't believe I used to spend all of Monday doing what this does in 10 seconds."

— Operations Manager, week 2 of parallel testing

What Changed

  • Added a "last updated" timestamp per metric after trust concerns in week 1.
  • Introduced a data source indicator so users could see where each number came from.
  • Simplified the filtering on the detail view — the original design had too many filter options that nobody used.

Business Impact

The dashboard eliminated the need to hire two additional ops staff that had been budgeted for Q3. The freed-up time was redirected to proactive customer outreach, contributing to a 12% improvement in customer satisfaction scores over the following quarter.

User Impact

Ops managers reported significantly higher job satisfaction. The shift from data entry to analysis work was consistently cited in team surveys as the biggest positive change of the year.

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have involved the executives more directly in the design process. We designed the executive view based on ops manager descriptions of what executives wanted — which turned out to be slightly different from what they actually needed. A few direct conversations earlier would have saved a redesign cycle.

What This Project Taught Me

That designing for internal tools requires the same rigor as external products. Internal users tolerate friction because they have no alternative — but that doesn't mean the friction is acceptable. This project showed me that operational UX can have direct, measurable business impact.

How It Shaped My Approach

This was my introduction to designing for data and efficiency rather than consumer aesthetics. It fundamentally changed how I think about value — a tool that saves 20 hours per week is more impactful than a beautiful interface that doesn't change behavior.
