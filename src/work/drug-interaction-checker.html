Sunset

Drug Interaction Checker

Designed a pharmacist-facing drug interaction tool that showed strong early adoption, but was eventually sunset due to regulatory changes that made the product's data model non-compliant.

Company

NovaPharma

Domain

Pharma

Product Type

B2B

Role

Sole Designer

Timeline

6 months (Jan – Jun 2021)

Team

1 PM, 2 Engineers, 1 Pharmacist (SME), 1 Designer

Drug interaction interface screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

Pharmacists at NovaPharma's partner pharmacies used outdated reference books and fragmented digital tools to check drug interactions. The process was slow, error-prone, and didn't scale — especially with the growing complexity of multi-drug regimens for elderly patients.

My Responsibility

Sole designer on a small, fast-moving team. Conducted pharmacist research, designed the interaction checker interface, severity classification system, and reporting workflow. Worked with a pharmacist SME to validate clinical accuracy of the UI patterns.

Outcome Summary

Successfully launched to 120+ pharmacies. Reduced average interaction check time from 4 minutes to 45 seconds. Strong user satisfaction. However, the product was sunset 8 months after launch when new FDA guidance changed data classification requirements, making the underlying data model non-compliant.

Key Impact Areas

Operational Efficiency Sunset

Context & Background

Business Context

NovaPharma was expanding its digital health tools portfolio. The drug interaction checker was positioned as a value-add for their pharmacy partnerships — pharmacies would get the tool at no cost in exchange for using NovaPharma's supply chain. The tool was meant to be a differentiator in competitive partnership negotiations.

User Context

Pharmacists in retail and hospital settings processed 200-400 prescriptions daily. Interaction checks were required for complex regimens (5+ medications). The existing workflow involved cross-referencing a commercial database, a printed reference guide, and sometimes calling the prescribing physician — all while the patient waited.

Constraints

The drug interaction data was licensed from a third-party database with specific display requirements. FDA regulations governed how interaction severity could be communicated. The tool needed to work offline (many pharmacies had unreliable internet). The pharmacist SME was only available part-time.

Why This Mattered

Drug interaction errors are a significant patient safety concern. A faster, more accurate checking tool could prevent adverse events while also saving pharmacists time — a win-win that would strengthen NovaPharma's pharmacy partnerships.

Problem Framing

Problem Statement

"How might we give pharmacists a fast, accurate, and compliant way to check drug interactions — so they can make confident decisions without slowing down the dispensing workflow?"

Key Assumptions

  • Speed was the primary adoption driver — if the tool wasn't faster than the current process, pharmacists wouldn't switch.
  • Severity classification needed to be immediately scannable (no reading required for routine checks).
  • Offline capability was non-negotiable for hospital pharmacies.
  • Pharmacists would trust the tool if they could see the data source and last-updated date.

Risks

  • Regulatory requirements for drug information display were complex and evolving.
  • The licensed data might not cover all interactions, creating false confidence.
  • Pharmacists might over-rely on the tool and reduce their own clinical judgment.

Success Criteria

Primary: 80%+ adoption at partner pharmacies within 3 months. Secondary: reduce average interaction check time by 50%+. Stretch: zero reported cases of missed critical interactions.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

Pharmacy Site Visits (6) Pharmacist Interviews (12) Workflow Shadowing Regulatory Review

Visited 6 pharmacies (3 retail, 3 hospital) to observe real dispensing workflows. Interviewed 12 pharmacists about their interaction checking habits. Shadowed 8 complete prescription processing sessions. Reviewed FDA and state board guidelines with our pharmacist SME.

Key Insights

Insight 1: Pharmacists wanted "traffic light" severity, not detailed classifications.

For routine checks, they needed to know: red (stop, call doctor), yellow (caution, document), green (proceed). Detailed severity levels were only needed for complex cases.

Insight 2: The bottleneck was drug name entry, not the interaction database.

Typing drug names (especially generics) was slow and error-prone. Pharmacists wanted barcode scanning or NDC number lookup.

Insight 3: Pharmacists needed to document their interaction checks for audit trails.

Many were printing screens or writing notes. An integrated documentation workflow would save significant time.

Strategy & Decision Making

Design Approach

Designed for the 80% case (routine checks) while supporting the 20% case (complex multi-drug regimens). The default view was a fast, scannable severity summary. Detailed clinical information was available one click deeper for pharmacists who needed it.

Key Decisions

  • Traffic light severity model: Reduced 5-level severity to 3 visible levels with detail expandable on demand. Validated with pharmacist SME and regulatory team.
  • NDC barcode scanning: Added barcode scanning as the primary input method, with text search as fallback. This reduced entry time from 15 seconds to 2 seconds per drug.
  • Integrated documentation: Built a one-click "document and proceed" workflow that automatically logged the interaction check, pharmacist decision, and timestamp.
  • Offline-first architecture: Drug database synced nightly, with interaction checks running locally. This was a significant engineering investment but non-negotiable for hospital pharmacies.

Design Execution

Interaction Checker Interface

Designed a two-panel layout: left panel for drug entry (scan or search), right panel for real-time interaction results. Results appeared instantly as drugs were added, with severity-coded cards that could be expanded for clinical detail.

Interaction checker — main view
Severity detail expanded

Documentation Flow

Designed a contextual documentation panel that appeared after interaction review. Pharmacists could select from predefined actions (proceed, modify dose, contact prescriber, reject), add optional notes, and submit — all without leaving the checker screen.

Offline Mode

Designed clear status indicators showing online/offline state and database freshness. When offline, the interface looked identical but displayed a subtle "Last synced: [date]" indicator. When the database was more than 48 hours old, a prominent warning appeared.

Validation & Iteration

Testing

Piloted with 15 pharmacies for 6 weeks. Pharmacists used the tool alongside their existing workflow to validate accuracy and build trust. Zero critical interaction misses were reported during the pilot.

Iteration

  • Added a "frequent combinations" feature after observing pharmacists checking the same drug pairs repeatedly.
  • Redesigned the barcode scanning error state after 20% of scans failed on faded labels.
  • Simplified the documentation flow from 3 clicks to 1 for the most common action (proceed with no modifications).

Feedback

"I used to dread the complex interaction checks. Now it takes me less time to check 8 drugs than it used to take for 3."

— Hospital Pharmacist, pilot week 4

Business Impact (Before Sunset)

The tool was successfully used as a partnership differentiator, contributing to 8 new pharmacy partnership signings worth $2.1M in annual supply chain revenue. Pharmacist adoption exceeded the 80% target within 2 months.

◼ Product Sunset

What Happened

Eight months after launch, the FDA issued updated guidance on drug interaction data classification that fundamentally changed how severity levels could be communicated to healthcare professionals. Our traffic light model — the core of the product's UX — became non-compliant overnight.

Why It Was Sunset (Not Updated)

  • The new FDA guidance required a 7-level severity classification with mandatory clinical evidence citations — fundamentally different from our 3-level model.
  • The licensed data provider couldn't supply data in the new format for 6+ months.
  • Rebuilding the data model, re-validating with our pharmacist SME, and re-certifying would take 8-10 months — by which time competitors would have compliant tools in market.
  • NovaPharma made the business decision to sunset rather than invest in a product that would arrive late to a changed market.

What Was Preserved

The design patterns (barcode scanning, contextual documentation, offline-first architecture) were adopted by NovaPharma's inventory management team. The research findings and pharmacist relationships informed 2 subsequent products. The sunset was executed cleanly — partners received 90-day notice, data was migrated, and no pharmacist was left without a working tool.

What I Learned

  • In regulated industries, the rules can change faster than your product can adapt. Building for compliance-flexibility (not just current compliance) is essential.
  • A product's value isn't determined solely by its runtime. The research, patterns, and relationships from a sunset product can have lasting impact.
  • Sunsetting well is a design skill. The transition plan for pharmacies was itself a design problem worth solving carefully.

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have designed the severity classification system to be more configurable from the start. We hard-coded the traffic light model because it tested well with users, but a more flexible data model could have adapted to the FDA change. This taught me that in regulated domains, your design system needs a "compliance abstraction layer."

What This Project Taught Me

That building a genuinely useful product that gets sunset isn't failure — it's reality. External forces (regulation, market shifts, business strategy) can end a product regardless of quality. The measure of the work isn't just "did it survive" but "did it create value while it existed, and did the learnings carry forward?"

How It Shaped My Approach

I now always ask early in regulated projects: "What happens if the rules change?" This isn't pessimism — it's design maturity. I also learned that sunsetting a product is a user experience that deserves the same care as launching one.
