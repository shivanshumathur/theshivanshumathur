Advisor Portfolio Platform

Designed an advisor-facing fintech platform that improved portfolio data entry efficiency by 25% and streamlined compliance workflows for financial advisors.

Company

FinServe

Domain

Fintech

Product Type

B2B

Role

Senior Designer

Timeline

7 months (Mar – Sep 2022)

Team

1 PM, 3 Engineers, 1 Compliance, 1 Designer

Platform screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

Financial advisors at FinServe spent excessive time on manual data entry and compliance checks when managing client portfolios. The existing system was built for back-office operations, not for the advisor's daily workflow. Errors in data entry led to compliance flags that took days to resolve.

My Responsibility

Led design strategy and execution. Conducted extensive research with financial advisors in the field. Designed the core portfolio management interface, compliance check integration, and batch data entry flows. Collaborated with legal/compliance to ensure all designs met regulatory requirements (SEC/FINRA).

Outcome Summary

25% improvement in data entry efficiency. Compliance-related escalations decreased by 40%. Advisor satisfaction scores improved from 3.2 to 4.1 (out of 5). Platform became the most-used tool during client meetings.

Key Impact Areas

Operational Efficiency Revenue Growth Retention / Engagement

Context & Background

Business Context

FinServe provided portfolio management tools to independent financial advisors. Their platform had grown through acquisitions, resulting in a fragmented UX with 3 different design systems coexisting. Advisor churn was increasing at 8% annually, with "ease of use" cited as the #2 reason for switching (after pricing).

User Context

Users were licensed financial advisors managing 50-300 client accounts each. They needed to enter trade data, run compliance checks, and generate client reports — often during or immediately after client meetings. Their tolerance for friction was low because every minute spent on admin was a minute not spent with clients.

Constraints

SEC and FINRA regulations mandated specific data fields and audit trails. Any change to data entry flows required compliance review. The backend was a distributed system built from 3 acquired products, so data consistency was an ongoing engineering challenge.

Why This Mattered

Reducing churn by even 2% would save $1.8M in annual revenue. More importantly, a better advisor experience could become a competitive differentiator in a market where most tools were equally clunky.

Problem Framing

Problem Statement

"How might we streamline the advisor's daily workflow — from trade entry to compliance — so they spend less time on admin and more time with clients?"

Key Assumptions

  • Advisors would adopt a new interface if it saved them measurable time.
  • Inline compliance validation (rather than batch checking) would catch errors earlier.
  • A unified design language would reduce cognitive load across different platform sections.
  • Batch data entry capabilities would significantly reduce repetitive work for multi-account advisors.

Risks

  • Advisors might resist any change to their established workflows, regardless of improvement.
  • Compliance team might slow down design iteration with lengthy review cycles.
  • The fragmented backend might not support the unified UX vision without significant engineering investment.

Success Criteria

Primary: 20%+ improvement in trade entry speed. Secondary: 30%+ reduction in compliance-related errors. Stretch: measurable improvement in advisor retention at 12 months.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

Field Visits (4 advisory firms) User Interviews (15) Task Analysis Support Ticket Analysis

Visited 4 advisory firms to observe advisors using the platform during actual work hours. Interviewed 15 advisors with varying portfolio sizes (50 to 300+ accounts). Performed task analysis on the 5 most frequent workflows. Analyzed 6 months of support tickets related to data entry and compliance errors.

Key Insights

Insight 1: Advisors entered the same data 3x for a single trade.

Data moved between the trading system, compliance check, and client reporting — each requiring manual re-entry because the systems didn't share data.

Insight 2: Compliance errors were design problems, not user problems.

73% of compliance flags could have been prevented with inline validation. The system let advisors submit invalid data and only caught it hours later in batch processing.

Insight 3: Advisors developed elaborate workarounds.

Many used personal spreadsheets as a "staging area" before entering data into the platform. They'd batch their entries at end of day rather than entering in real time, creating a 24-hour data lag.

How Insights Influenced Direction

The triple-entry problem made batch operations and data flow unification our top priority. The compliance insight shifted us from "post-submission validation" to "inline prevention." The workaround finding showed us that advisors were already thinking in batches — so we designed a batch-first interface rather than forcing one-at-a-time entry.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Full platform unification

Pros: unified experience, clean architecture. Cons: 18+ month timeline, massive engineering investment, high risk.

Rejected — timeline unrealistic for the churn problem.

Option B: Redesign the 5 core workflows with shared data layer Selected

Pros: focused impact, achievable in 7 months, addresses the top pain points. Cons: inconsistency in lesser-used areas persists.

Selected — targeted high-impact workflows while planning for broader unification.

Option C: API-level data sharing only (no UI changes)

Pros: eliminates triple entry. Cons: doesn't address UX issues, inline compliance, or navigation confusion.

Rejected — necessary but insufficient; bundled into Option B.

Key Trade-offs

  • Speed vs. comprehensiveness: Focused on 5 workflows that covered 80% of daily advisor activity. Other areas would follow in later phases.
  • Inline validation vs. existing compliance process: Required 3 months of negotiation with the compliance team to allow real-time validation without removing the batch audit trail.
  • Batch entry vs. single-record UX: Designed primarily for batch workflows, with single-record entry as the secondary path. This reversed the original product's paradigm.

Design Execution

Core Workflows

Redesigned the trade entry, portfolio rebalancing, compliance check, client report generation, and account onboarding flows. Each was designed to support both single-record and batch operations with a consistent interaction pattern.

Workflow comparison diagram placeholder

Batch Entry Interface

Designed a spreadsheet-inspired batch entry view that felt familiar to advisors who were already using spreadsheets as workarounds. Added inline validation with real-time compliance indicators — green/yellow/red status on each row before submission.

Batch entry — desktop view
Inline compliance indicators

Visual Design & System

Established a unified design system across the 5 redesigned workflows — consistent typography, spacing, form patterns, and navigation. Used a dense but organized layout optimized for desktop (96% of advisor sessions were desktop).

Validation & Iteration

Testing Approach

Ran 3 rounds of usability testing with 5 advisors per round using real (anonymized) portfolio data. Beta launched with 20 advisory firms for 6 weeks before general release. Measured time-on-task, error rates, and qualitative satisfaction.

What Changed

  • Added keyboard shortcuts for batch entry after advisors in Round 1 requested them (Tab, Enter, arrow key navigation).
  • Redesigned the compliance indicator from a small icon to a full-row color band after testers missed the subtle icon.
  • Added an "undo" capability for batch submissions — advisors were anxious about submitting 50+ entries at once without a safety net.

Feedback

"This is the first time the system actually works the way I think. I can enter a full day's trades in 20 minutes instead of an hour."

— Senior Financial Advisor, beta week 4

Business Impact

Advisor churn decreased from 8% to 5.5% in the 12 months following launch, with the redesigned workflows cited as a primary factor in retention surveys. The batch entry feature was highlighted in sales demos, contributing to 12 new firm acquisitions.

User Impact

Advisors stopped using personal spreadsheets as staging areas. Real-time data entry replaced end-of-day batching, eliminating the 24-hour data lag. Compliance teams reported spending 60% less time on error resolution.

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have included the compliance team in the design process from the start rather than treating them as a review gate. Their domain knowledge about common error patterns could have informed the inline validation design earlier, saving us a significant redesign cycle in month 4.

What This Project Taught Me

That designing for experts requires a different approach than designing for general consumers. Expert users value efficiency and power over simplicity. The spreadsheet-inspired batch interface was counter to "clean design" principles but perfectly aligned with how advisors actually thought about their work.

How It Shaped My Approach

This project solidified my belief in designing for real behavior rather than ideal behavior. Instead of trying to change how advisors worked, I designed a system that matched their mental model. The workarounds they'd built weren't problems to be eliminated — they were research data showing us the right design direction.
