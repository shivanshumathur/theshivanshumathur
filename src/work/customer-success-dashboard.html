Customer Success Dashboard

Designed a customer health scoring and success dashboard that gave CSMs proactive visibility into account health, contributing to a 15% improvement in net revenue retention.

Company

Opus SaaS

Domain

SaaS

Product Type

B2B

Role

Lead Designer

Timeline

6 months (Feb – Jul 2024)

Team

1 PM, 3 Engineers, 1 Data Scientist, 1 Designer

Dashboard screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

Opus SaaS's customer success managers (CSMs) had no centralized view of account health. They relied on gut feeling, scattered signals in Salesforce, and reactive escalations. Churn was often detected too late — by the time a customer was unhappy, the damage was done. Net revenue retention (NRR) had plateaued at 92%.

My Responsibility

Led design for the entire dashboard — from research with CSMs and leadership, through health score definition (with the data science team), to interface design, usability testing, and post-launch iteration. Also designed the alert and playbook system.

Outcome Summary

NRR improved from 92% to 106% within 9 months. CSMs shifted from reactive firefighting to proactive engagement. Churn prediction accuracy reached 78%. The dashboard became the primary daily tool for the 18-person CS team.

Key Impact Areas

Revenue Growth Retention / Engagement Operational Efficiency

Context & Background

Business Context

Opus SaaS served 800+ B2B customers across mid-market and enterprise segments. The company had shifted its growth strategy from acquisition to retention and expansion. The CS team had grown from 8 to 18 people in a year, but without proper tooling, the additional headcount wasn't translating to better retention outcomes.

User Context

CSMs managed 40-60 accounts each. Their daily workflow involved checking Salesforce for renewal dates, monitoring support tickets in Zendesk, tracking product usage in Amplitude, and reading Slack channels for escalations. There was no single place that showed "this account needs attention right now."

Constraints

Data was spread across 5 systems (Salesforce, Zendesk, Amplitude, billing, and the product database). The data science team was available half-time. CSMs were resistant to "yet another dashboard" — they'd been burned by internal tools before. The VP of CS needed to demonstrate ROI within 6 months to justify the investment.

Why This Mattered

At a $45K average contract value and 8% annual churn, Opus was losing approximately $2.9M in revenue annually. Improving NRR was the CEO's top priority for the year. The CS dashboard was the strategic bet to make that happen.

Problem Framing

Problem Statement

"How might we give CSMs a proactive, unified view of account health — so they can identify at-risk accounts early and take action before customers churn?"

Key Assumptions

  • A composite health score (combining usage, support, engagement, and commercial signals) would be more predictive than any single metric.
  • CSMs would act on health score changes if the dashboard provided clear next steps (not just data).
  • Early intervention (60+ days before renewal) would significantly improve retention outcomes.
  • CSMs needed a prioritized task list, not just a dashboard to monitor.

Risks

  • If the health score wasn't accurate, CSMs would lose trust and stop using the tool.
  • Too many alerts could create "alert fatigue" and be ignored.
  • The data pipeline might not be reliable enough for real-time health scoring.

Success Criteria

Primary: daily active usage by 80%+ of CSMs within 3 months. Secondary: NRR improvement of 5%+ within 6 months. Stretch: measurable reduction in time-to-intervention for at-risk accounts.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

CSM Interviews (12) Workflow Shadowing (5 CSMs) Churn Analysis (100 accounts) CS Leadership Interviews (3)

Interviewed 12 of 18 CSMs (varying tenure and portfolio sizes). Shadowed 5 CSMs for a full day each. Analyzed 100 churned accounts to identify patterns and early warning signals. Interviewed the VP of CS and 2 CS directors about their strategic needs.

Key Insights

Insight 1: CSMs already had a mental health score — it just wasn't formalized.

Every CSM could rank their accounts from healthiest to most at-risk. But these rankings lived in their heads, weren't shared, and weren't data-driven.

Insight 2: The biggest churn predictor was a decline in executive engagement, not product usage.

Accounts where the executive sponsor stopped attending QBRs were 3x more likely to churn — regardless of product usage levels.

Insight 3: CSMs wanted playbooks, not just data.

"Show me a red score and I'll feel anxious. Show me a red score with 3 things I can do about it, and I'll feel empowered." Actionability was the key adoption driver.

How Insights Influenced Direction

The mental model insight validated that CSMs would adopt a scoring system — they already thought in health scores, we just needed to formalize and share them. The executive engagement finding changed our health score formula significantly (we weighted engagement signals higher than usage). The playbook insight became a core feature, not an afterthought.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Buy Gainsight or Totango

Pros: feature-rich, proven at scale. Cons: $150K+/year, 4-6 month implementation, limited customization of health score model.

Rejected — too expensive for the stage and too rigid for our health model needs.

Option B: Build a focused internal dashboard Selected

Pros: custom health score, playbook integration, built for our specific workflow. Cons: 6-month build, requires data engineering investment.

Selected — gave us full control over the health model and CSM experience.

Option C: Enhance Salesforce with custom reports

Pros: already in the tech stack, fast to implement. Cons: limited visualization, no real-time scoring, CSMs already disliked working in Salesforce.

Rejected — would reinforce existing frustrations rather than solving them.

Key Trade-offs

  • ML-driven score vs. rule-based score: Started with a rule-based health score (transparent, explainable) with a plan to layer ML predictions once we had enough data. CSMs needed to understand why a score was what it was.
  • Comprehensive data vs. available data: Launched with 4 data sources (usage, support, commercial, engagement) even though we wanted 6. Perfect data would have delayed launch by 3 months.
  • Dashboard vs. task manager: Made the primary view a prioritized action list (not a dashboard of charts). CSMs needed to know "what to do today," not "how my portfolio looks in aggregate."

Design Execution

Health Score System

Designed a composite health score (0-100) with 4 weighted components: Product Usage (25%), Support Health (20%), Commercial Signals (30%), Engagement (25%). Each component was visible in a breakdown view so CSMs could see exactly what was driving the score. Used a 4-tier color system: green (80-100), yellow (60-79), orange (40-59), red (0-39).

Health score breakdown diagram placeholder

Primary View: Action Queue

The default view was a prioritized list of accounts that needed attention today — sorted by urgency (score drop velocity, renewal proximity, open escalations). Each item showed the health score, key signal, and a recommended action from the playbook library.

Action queue — CSM view
Account detail — health breakdown

Playbook System

Designed a contextual playbook system that surfaced recommended actions based on the specific health signals triggering a score change. For example: if executive engagement dropped, the playbook suggested "Schedule Executive Business Review" with email templates. If product usage declined, it suggested "Product adoption check-in" with specific feature recommendations.

Leadership View

Created a separate view for CS leadership showing portfolio-level health distribution, renewal forecast, and team workload balance. This view was explicitly not available to individual CSMs — we wanted them focused on action, not comparison.

Validation & Iteration

Testing Approach

Piloted with 6 CSMs for 4 weeks while validating health score accuracy against known account outcomes. Ran weekly feedback sessions to iterate on both the UI and the health score formula.

What Changed

  • Re-weighted the health score formula after pilot data showed commercial signals (expansion conversations, contract discussions) were more predictive than we initially thought — increased from 20% to 30%.
  • Added a "CSM override" capability after experienced CSMs requested the ability to flag accounts they knew were fine despite a low score (e.g., a customer doing a planned migration that temporarily reduced usage).
  • Simplified the playbook system from 20 playbooks to 8 — CSMs were overwhelmed by too many options. Focused on the most common and most impactful action types.
  • Moved the "portfolio health distribution" from the CSM view to leadership-only after CSMs reported feeling anxious comparing their portfolio health to peers.

Business Impact

NRR moved from 92% to 106% within 9 months (meaning the company was expanding revenue within its existing customer base, not just preventing churn). The dashboard was directly credited with saving 12 at-risk renewals worth $540K in the first 6 months. CS leadership cited it as the most impactful internal tool investment of the year.

User Impact

CSMs reported feeling more confident and proactive. The average time between a health score drop and first CSM outreach decreased from 14 days to 4.3 days (3.2x improvement). Team morale improved as CSMs shifted from reactive firefighting to strategic account management.

Qualitative Feedback

"The health score dashboard is the first tool we've built internally that the team actually loves using. It's changed how we operate."

— VP of Customer Success, 6-month review

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have built the CSM override feature from day one. We under-estimated how important it was for CSMs to maintain agency over their accounts. The initial version felt like the system was telling them what to think — the override transformed it into a tool that supported their judgment.

What This Project Taught Me

That the best internal tools are opinionated about workflows, not just informative about data. The shift from "dashboard" to "action queue" was the single most important design decision. CSMs didn't need to see their portfolio in aggregate — they needed to know what to do next. This reframing changed everything.

How It Shaped My Approach

This project reinforced that adoption is the ultimate measure of internal tool success. Every design decision was filtered through "will this make CSMs want to open this tool every morning?" If the answer was no, we cut it. This ruthless focus on adoption over feature completeness is something I now bring to every internal tool project.
