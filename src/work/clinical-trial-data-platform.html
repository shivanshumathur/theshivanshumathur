Clinical Trial Data Platform

Built a data ingestion and visualization platform for clinical trial managers to track patient enrollment and site performance in real time.

Company

PharmaSolutions Ltd.

Domain

Pharma

Product Type

Internal Platform

Role

Lead Designer

Timeline

8 months (Sep 2019 – Apr 2020)

Team

1 PM, 4 Engineers, 1 Data Analyst, 1 Designer

Platform screenshot placeholder

Quick Overview

Problem Summary

Clinical trial managers at PharmaSolutions were tracking patient enrollment, site performance, and regulatory milestones across 15+ trials using disconnected spreadsheets and email threads. Enrollment delays were detected weeks late, costing the company millions in extended trial timelines.

My Responsibility

Led design from discovery through launch. Conducted research with trial managers and medical directors. Designed the complete platform including data visualization, alerting system, and design system foundations. Partnered closely with the data engineering team on pipeline architecture.

Outcome Summary

Reduced enrollment delay detection from weeks to hours. Platform became the primary tool for 40+ trial managers across 3 offices. Design system components were reused across 3 subsequent products.

Key Impact Areas

Revenue Growth Automation / Cost Reduction

Context & Background

Business Context

PharmaSolutions managed clinical trials for pharmaceutical companies. Each delayed trial day cost sponsors $20,000–$100,000 depending on the phase. The company's competitive advantage depended on faster, more transparent trial management — but their internal tooling was years behind.

User Context

Trial managers coordinated across 5-20 research sites per trial. They needed to track enrollment targets, monitor protocol deviations, and report to sponsors weekly. Most were experienced professionals with domain expertise but limited technical fluency — they needed clarity, not complexity.

Constraints

Strict regulatory requirements around data handling (GxP compliance). Data sources were heterogeneous — some sites used electronic systems, others submitted paper forms. The engineering team was building the data pipeline in parallel with the UI, creating dependencies and unknowns throughout the project.

Why This Mattered

Three major sponsor contracts were up for renewal, and all three had expressed frustration with reporting delays. If PharmaSolutions couldn't demonstrate better trial visibility, they risked losing $4M+ in annual revenue.

Problem Framing

Problem Statement

"How might we give trial managers real-time visibility into enrollment progress and site performance — so delays are detected early and sponsor reporting becomes effortless?"

Key Assumptions

  • Trial managers would adopt a new tool if it reduced their reporting burden.
  • Automated data ingestion could handle the variety of source formats.
  • Visualizing enrollment trends would surface problems earlier than spreadsheet reviews.
  • Sponsors would value transparency even when the news was bad.

Risks

  • Data quality from paper-based sites could undermine trust in the platform.
  • Regulatory review could delay launch beyond the sponsor renewal deadline.
  • Building the pipeline and UI simultaneously meant requirements were shifting.

Success Criteria

Primary: detect enrollment delays within 48 hours instead of 2-3 weeks. Secondary: reduce weekly sponsor report preparation from 8 hours to under 1 hour. Stretch: retain all three at-risk sponsor contracts.

Research & Insights

Research Methods

Stakeholder Interviews (10) Workflow Shadowing Competitor Analysis Data Audit

Interviewed 10 trial managers across 3 offices. Shadowed 2 complete weekly reporting cycles. Audited existing data sources to understand quality and completeness. Analyzed 3 competitor platforms to understand industry benchmarks.

Key Insights

Insight 1: Trial managers needed alerts, not dashboards.

They didn't want to monitor a dashboard all day — they wanted to be notified when something deviated from plan.

Insight 2: Sponsor reporting was the real pain point — not internal tracking.

Most of the 8 hours per week went into formatting data for sponsor-specific report templates, not analyzing it.

Insight 3: Trust required showing data sources and freshness.

Trial managers wouldn't trust a number unless they could trace it back to the source system and see when it was last updated.

How Insights Influenced Direction

The alert insight led us to build a notification system as a core feature, not an add-on. The sponsor reporting pain shifted our priority from "beautiful dashboard" to "exportable, sponsor-ready reports." The trust requirement resulted in data provenance indicators on every metric.

Strategy & Decision Making

Options Considered

Option A: Enhance existing spreadsheets with automation

Pros: familiar workflow, fast. Cons: wouldn't scale, no real-time capability, data silos remain.

Rejected — doesn't solve the fundamental visibility problem.

Option B: Purpose-built platform with alert system and export Selected

Pros: solves core problems, scalable, competitive differentiator. Cons: 8-month build, parallel pipeline dependency.

Selected — aligned with business urgency and long-term strategy.

Option C: License a CTMS (Clinical Trial Management System)

Pros: industry standard, feature-rich. Cons: $500K+ implementation, 12+ month timeline, limited customization.

Rejected — too expensive and slow for the timeline.

Key Trade-offs

  • Build incrementally vs. launch complete: Shipped enrollment tracking first, then added site performance and reporting features in two subsequent releases.
  • Perfect data vs. good-enough data: Accepted 90% data accuracy from paper-based sites with visible confidence indicators, rather than delaying launch for 100% accuracy.
  • GxP compliance vs. speed: Worked with the regulatory team to define a compliant-enough MVP that could be hardened post-launch.

Validation & Iteration

Testing Approach

Pilot launched with 5 trial managers on 3 active trials for 4 weeks before full rollout. Collected structured feedback weekly and tracked actual usage via analytics.

What Changed

  • Redesigned the alert threshold configuration after pilot users found it confusing — simplified from custom ranges to predefined severity levels.
  • Added a "data quality score" per site after trial managers requested a way to assess how reliable each site's data was.
  • Built a quick-export button for the enrollment summary — users were taking screenshots instead of using the full export flow.

Feedback

— Senior Trial Manager, pilot week 3

Business Impact

All three at-risk sponsor contracts were renewed, with the platform cited as a key differentiator. The company started including platform access as a selling point in new business pitches, contributing to two new contract wins worth $1.2M annually.

User Impact

Trial managers shifted from reactive firefighting to proactive management. The alert system caught two critical enrollment delays in the first month that would have gone unnoticed for weeks under the old process.

Reflection

What I Would Do Differently

I would have brought sponsor-facing users into the research earlier. We designed the export flow based on trial manager descriptions of what sponsors wanted — but when sponsors actually saw the reports, they requested different formatting and metrics. Adding a sponsor interview round would have saved rework.

What This Project Taught Me

That in regulated industries, "good enough" data with clear provenance is more valuable than perfect data that arrives late. This project taught me to design for trust and transparency — showing users where numbers come from is as important as showing the numbers themselves.

How It Shaped My Approach

This was where I learned to design for complex data environments. I now always ask: "what's the source, how fresh is it, and how confident should the user be?" These questions have become fundamental to how I approach any data-heavy product.
