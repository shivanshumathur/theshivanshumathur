Failure & Retrospectives 10 min read · Jul 3, 2025

What a Rolled-Back Product Taught Me

Related project: Checkout Flow Redesign →

Context

In mid-2024, I led the design for a checkout flow simplification at RetailX. The project had strong executive buy-in, clear metrics, and encouraging usability test results. We shipped it to 100% of traffic after a short A/B test. Two weeks later, we rolled it back. Conversion had dropped. This is a reflection on what went wrong and what I learned.

The project looked right at every checkpoint

We had a clear problem statement: the existing checkout had too many steps, and analytics showed significant drop-off between cart and confirmation. Research supported a simplified flow — users told us the process felt tedious. We removed two steps, consolidated inputs, and tested the prototype with twelve users. All completed the flow faster. Task success was 100%.

The A/B test looked promising too. Over the first three days, the new flow showed marginally better completion rates. We made the call to roll out.

What actually happened

Once we hit 100% traffic, conversion dropped by 4.2% over two weeks. Support tickets increased — users were confused by the consolidated fields, particularly around shipping vs. billing addresses. The "simpler" flow was actually harder for a segment of users who didn't fit our research profile.

The A/B test had been too short, the sample too small, and — critically — it didn't include return customers, who made up the majority of actual purchasers. We'd tested on a population that didn't represent the real user base.

What I got wrong

  • I conflated test success with product success. A 100% task completion rate in a lab setting doesn't predict behavior at scale. Context, habits, and edge cases dominate in production.
  • I underweighted the existing mental model. Users had learned the old flow. Changing it introduced friction even if the new version was objectively "simpler." Switching costs are real.
  • I didn't push back on the A/B test scope. I knew the sample was small and the timeline was tight, but I went along with it because the stakeholders were eager. That's on me.
  • I assumed "less steps = better." That's a heuristic, not a law. In this case, separate steps gave users a sense of control. Consolidation felt rushed and error-prone.

What changed my mind

Before this project, I leaned heavily on usability testing as the gold standard for design validation. After this, I treat it as one signal among many. Post-launch data, cohort analysis, and behavioral segmentation are just as important — and sometimes more reliable than controlled tests.

I also became much more willing to advocate for longer A/B test cycles, even when it's politically inconvenient. A bad rollout costs more than a delayed launch.

What I would do differently now

  • Run the A/B test for at least two full purchase cycles (in this case, 4+ weeks) and ensure the sample included return customers.
  • Phase the rollout: start at 10%, then 25%, then 50% — with monitoring gates at each stage.
  • Include a "rollback plan" as a first-class artifact in the project plan, not an afterthought.
  • Challenge my own confidence when usability tests are too clean. Real-world behavior is messy; if tests aren't showing any friction, something might be missing from the test design.

How this shows up in my work now

Every project I've worked on since has included a more rigorous validation plan. I'm more skeptical of clean results, more insistent on representative samples, and more comfortable recommending that we wait before scaling a launch. This failure made me a better designer — not because failure is romantic, but because it forced me to update my process where it was weakest.
