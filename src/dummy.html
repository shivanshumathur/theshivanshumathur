Conversational RAG Chatbot

Designing an AI-powered conversational assistant built on retrieval-augmented generation to help finance teams query cost data, generate reports, and surface insights through natural language.

Role Sr. UX Strategist
Team 3 designers, 8 engineers, 2 PMs
Duration 6 months
Domain Enterprise AI · Finance
Context

Novartis Digital Finance manages cost planning, analytics, and forecasting across multiple business units globally. Finance analysts were spending 3–4 hours daily navigating complex dashboards and running manual SQL queries to extract insights from cost data. Leadership needed a faster, self-serve way for non-technical users to access financial intelligence.

Problem

The existing BI tools were powerful but designed for data analysts — not finance managers or business partners. Users had to remember exact table names, filter hierarchies, and report structures. Over 60% of data requests were routed through a central analytics team, creating bottlenecks of 2–5 business days per request. There was no way to simply "ask a question" and get an answer.

Strategy & Approach

I led the UX strategy for what we internally called "FinChat" — a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) chatbot that combined an LLM layer with a live connection to Novartis finance databases. The approach:

  • Conducted 18 user interviews with finance analysts, controllers, and business partners to map query patterns and pain points
  • Defined the conversational UX framework — how the bot asks for clarification, handles ambiguity, and presents structured vs. freeform answers
  • Designed the "trust layer" — source citations, confidence indicators, and data freshness badges so users know what they're looking at
  • Created guardrails for sensitive data: role-based access, redaction rules, and audit trails for every query
  • Built a progressive disclosure model — simple answer first, drill-down on demand, export to Excel/PDF when needed
Key Design Decisions

Conversational, not dashboard. We deliberately avoided recreating another dashboard. The primary interaction is a chat input — users type questions like "What was our Q3 cost variance for oncology?" and get a structured response with a chart, table, and natural language summary.

Citations as trust anchors. Every response includes inline citations linking back to the source data table, last refresh timestamp, and a confidence score. Users told us they wouldn't trust an AI answer without knowing "where it came from."

Guided prompts over blank canvas. Instead of a blank chat input, we surface contextual prompt suggestions based on the user's role, recent queries, and current reporting cycle. This reduced the "I don't know what to ask" problem by 40%.

Failure states as learning moments. When the bot can't answer a query, it doesn't just say "I don't know." It shows what it understood, where it got stuck, and suggests a rephrased query — turning failures into teaching moments.

Process
  • Discovery research: shadowed 6 analysts over 2 weeks to understand real query workflows
  • Mapped 120+ common query patterns into a taxonomy of 8 intent categories
  • Wireframed 4 response type templates: single metric, comparison table, time series chart, and narrative summary
  • Ran 3 rounds of usability testing with 12 participants using Wizard-of-Oz prototypes before engineering build
  • Iterated the conversation flow 6 times based on real interaction logs from the beta
  • Collaborated with ML engineers to define prompt templates and retrieval ranking logic

Reflection

The hardest part wasn't the AI — it was the trust. Finance professionals are trained to verify every number. Convincing them to trust a chatbot's answer required showing our work at every step. The "citation layer" became the most praised feature in user feedback.

What I'd do differently: start with a smaller scope. We launched with 8 intent categories simultaneously. In hindsight, launching with 3 and proving accuracy would have built trust faster and reduced the initial error rate.
