Market Model

A strategic forecasting platform that replaced fragmented Excel models with a unified view of market dynamics — integrating epidemiology, pricing, and market share to support smarter planning decisions.

Role Senior UX Designer
Duration 17 months
Domain Enterprise · Digital Finance
Status Live
On this page
Role & Ownership

Sole UX designer on a 48-member cross-functional team (India, Spain, Switzerland). I owned the end-to-end design — from initial discovery with the Product Owner to information architecture, data visualization frameworks, interaction design, and usability validation across 4 regions. This was a ground-up build; there was no existing product. Excel was the product.

Core Problem

Strategic planning teams across Novartis were forecasting demand, pricing, and market share using dozens of disconnected Excel files — each owned by different individuals, with no version control, no audit trail, and no single source of truth. Manual reconciliation between global and local numbers took days. Knowledge lived in people, not systems. Scaling this globally was increasing risk and inconsistency with every planning cycle.

Key Decisions
One model, many lenses Instead of separate tools for demand forecasting, pricing, and competitive analysis, I designed a single unified model with switchable views — so users change context without switching tools.
Standardization without rigidity Every geography believed their process was unique. I designed a configurable template system that enforces a shared structure underneath while allowing local flexibility in data inputs and assumptions.
Data confidence scoring Not all inputs are equally reliable. I introduced confidence indicators on each data source so planners know whether they're building on solid ground or estimates — which became unexpectedly central to adoption.
Outcome & Impact
1 source
Single source of truth replacing fragmented Excel models across geographies
Real-time
Interactive scenario modeling replaced static Excel outputs
Standard
Consistent models across regions with local flexibility preserved
Trusted
Confidence scoring improved data trust and accelerated adoption
Key Learning

Forecasting tools aren't about numbers — they're about confidence. Users needed to trust the model before they'd trust the output. Standardization was politically harder than technically; the key was designing a system flexible enough to accommodate local needs while quietly enforcing a shared structure underneath.

CF/CC Portal

A centralized reporting solution for Central Functions & Central Cost that replaced fragmented financial structures with standardized, efficient global–local alignment.

Role Sr. UX Strategist
Duration 6 months
Domain Enterprise Platform · Digital Finance
Type Workflow Automation
On this page
Context

Novartis's Central Functions & Central Cost (CF/CC) unit needed to overhaul how financial reporting was structured. The previous "Embedded EG&A" reporting model had become decentralized and complex, creating misalignment between global and local financial reporting. The initiative aimed to replace this with a more centralized CF&CC reporting solution that would bring clarity, efficiency, and consistency to financial management.

Problem

The existing reporting structures were deeply fragmented. Global and local financial reporting were misaligned, making consolidation difficult. Processes were inefficient, consuming significant time and resources without clear returns. There was no standardization in data hierarchy or submission templates, and managing carve-outs and updates in cost and profit centers was an ongoing operational headache. P&L sales submissions and carve-outs regularly fell out of sync.

Strategy & Approach

The strategy focused on consolidating financial reporting into a single, unified solution that bridges the gap between global structure and local execution:

  • Mapped the existing reporting workflows across multiple organizational units to identify redundancies, gaps, and misalignment points
  • Designed a centralized portal that standardizes the submission process while accommodating local reporting requirements
  • Created standardized templates for key submissions including RLT (Regional Leadership Team) and C&G (Cost & Governance) reports
  • Designed the hierarchy mapping interface for SAP BPC integration, ensuring clean data flows between systems
  • Built workflows that align P&L sales submissions with carve-out timelines to prevent drift
Key Design Decisions

Templates over freeform. The biggest source of inconsistency was that every team created reports differently. We designed standardized submission templates that enforce structure while remaining flexible enough for regional nuances.

Visual hierarchy mapping. The SAP BPC hierarchy is notoriously complex. We designed a visual mapper that lets users see and edit cost/profit center relationships without navigating raw SAP interfaces — reducing errors and onboarding time.

Deadline-aware workflows. Submissions that miss alignment windows cause cascading issues. We built deadline visibility directly into the interface — showing upcoming windows, dependent submissions, and real-time status across teams.

Progressive complexity. Finance leads see summaries. Analysts can drill into detail. The same data, surfaced at the right depth for the right role.

Process
  • Discovery: conducted workshops with global finance controllers and regional leads to document current-state workflows
  • Mapped 8 distinct submission workflows and identified 23 points of misalignment between global and local processes
  • Designed and iterated on the portal IA — tested 2 navigation models with finance teams across 3 regions
  • Prototyped the SAP BPC hierarchy mapper and validated it with data management teams
  • Ran UAT sessions with finance controllers to ensure template standardization didn't break local reporting needs
  • Delivered the portal in 3 phased releases over 6 months
Outcome & Impact
Simplified
Reporting — consolidated fragmented structures into one unified solution
Aligned
Global–local reporting now synchronized with standardized templates
Efficient
Streamlined processes reduced time and resources spent on reporting
Better
Data hierarchy management through visual SAP BPC mapper
Reflection

This project taught me that reporting tools are really change management projects in disguise. The technical solution was straightforward — the challenge was getting dozens of teams across regions to adopt a new way of working. The key was making the new process visibly easier than the old one, not just theoretically better.

The visual hierarchy mapper turned out to be the feature that sold the whole portal. What was previously a painful, error-prone SAP task became something people could actually understand and manage directly.

Dost

An AI-powered Digital Finance Assistant that helps users navigate finance tools, resolve queries, and access centralized knowledge — all through a conversational RAG-based interface.

Role Sr. UX Strategist
Duration 5 months
Domain Conversational AI · Digital Finance
Type AI Chatbot · Knowledge Management
On this page
Context

Novartis Digital Finance operates a growing ecosystem of tools — Genie, Sofia, Market Model, BPC, and more. Finance professionals were heavily reliant on manual support channels to navigate these tools, resolve queries, and find process documentation. With no single point of access, users wasted time switching between systems and waiting for responses from support teams.

Problem

Finance teams across Novartis faced a fragmented support experience. Common, repetitive issues consumed significant bandwidth from both users and the support team. Knowledge sources were scattered without a centralized access point, and there was no feedback loop for continuous improvement. Users needed quick, context-aware guidance — not another manual or static FAQ.

Strategy & Approach

I led the UX strategy for Dost — an AI-powered conversational assistant built on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) that consolidates knowledge from all Digital Finance tools into a single, dynamic interface:

  • Mapped user pain points across multiple finance tools to identify the most common query types and support bottlenecks
  • Defined the conversational UX framework — how Dost handles ambiguity, provides contextual suggestions, and routes to the right tool
  • Designed the trust layer — source citations, confidence indicators, and data freshness badges so users know exactly where answers come from
  • Built a progressive disclosure model — quick answer first, deeper context on demand, with links to full documentation
  • Created a feedback loop mechanism so every interaction improves future responses
Key Design Decisions

One interface for many tools. Instead of building separate help systems for each finance tool, Dost acts as a unified assistant. Users ask one question and Dost figures out which tool, process, or knowledge base to pull from.

Guided prompts over blank canvas. Rather than an empty chat input, Dost surfaces contextual prompt suggestions based on the user's role, recent activity, and the current reporting cycle. This reduced the "I don't know what to ask" problem significantly.

Citations as trust anchors. Every response includes inline citations linking to the source — whether it's a Confluence page, a BPC guide, or a Genie tutorial. Finance professionals don't trust answers without provenance.

Failure states as learning moments. When Dost can't answer, it shows what it understood, where it got stuck, and suggests a rephrased query or escalation path — turning dead ends into productive next steps.

Process
  • Discovery research: interviewed finance analysts, controllers, and support staff to map common query patterns and pain points
  • Mapped 100+ repetitive queries into intent categories spanning tool navigation, process guidance, and troubleshooting
  • Wireframed response templates: short answer, step-by-step guide, tool redirect, and escalation
  • Ran usability testing with Wizard-of-Oz prototypes before engineering build
  • Iterated conversation flows based on real interaction logs from beta testing
  • Collaborated with ML engineers on prompt engineering, retrieval ranking, and knowledge ingestion pipelines
Outcome & Impact
Faster
Query resolution — from hours to seconds for common finance questions
↑
Improved productivity by reducing manual dependency on support teams
1 hub
Centralized knowledge access across Genie, Sofia, Market Model, BPC, and more
↑
Enhanced adoption of digital finance tools through guided onboarding
Reflection

The hardest part wasn't the AI — it was earning trust. Finance professionals verify every number. Convincing them to rely on a chatbot required transparency at every step. The citation layer became the most praised feature in user feedback.

Building Dost also revealed how fragmented the internal knowledge landscape was. The project became as much about knowledge consolidation as it was about AI. The feedback loop mechanism turned out to be critical — every unanswered question improved the system for the next user.

OnePSP

A data-driven Patient Support Program dashboard that visualizes real-time patient journey insights — helping healthcare teams improve access, track adherence, and deliver timely interventions.

Role UX Designer
Duration 11 months
Domain Healthcare Analytics · Patient Experience
Type Enterprise UX · Data Visualization
On this page
Context

Novartis runs Patient Support Programs (PSPs) across multiple regions to help patients access, stay on, and benefit from their treatments. These programs generate vast amounts of data — enrolment numbers, engagement metrics, adherence patterns, geographic distribution — but there was no unified way to visualize or act on this information. Stakeholders across medical, commercial, and operations teams each had fragmented, inconsistent views of program performance.

Problem

The lack of unified visibility was the core issue. Stakeholders couldn't track patient adoption, engagement, or adherence across regions in one place. Medicine usage trends and program effectiveness were difficult to assess with fragmented data sources. Reporting was manual and inconsistent, leading to delayed interventions when patients showed signs of drop-off. Evaluating the ROI of PSP initiatives required assembling data from multiple systems — a process that took days rather than minutes.

Strategy & Approach

OnePSP was designed to be the single patient-centric dashboard that aligns all stakeholders around the same data:

  • Conducted stakeholder interviews across medical, commercial, and operations teams to define the key metrics each group needs
  • Mapped the patient journey from enrolment through ongoing adherence to identify critical intervention points
  • Designed a multi-layered dashboard — executive summary for leadership, detailed drill-downs for program managers, geographic views for regional teams
  • Built real-time data visualization for engagement, enrolment trends, adherence patterns, and geographic distribution
  • Created early-warning indicators that flag patients at risk of drop-off before it happens
Key Design Decisions

Patient journey as the organizing principle. Instead of organizing the dashboard by data type (enrolment, adherence, engagement), we organized it around the patient journey. Every metric is anchored to a stage in the patient's experience — making the data meaningful rather than abstract.

Early signals over lagging indicators. Most reporting in PSP was retrospective — you'd find out about drop-off after it happened. We designed predictive indicators that surface risk signals early, giving teams time to intervene before patients disengage.

Geographic intelligence. Patient behavior varies significantly by state and channel. We built an interactive map layer that lets teams compare regional performance, identify underserved areas, and allocate resources based on real-time data.

Role-based views. A medical lead doesn't need the same dashboard as an operations manager. We designed role-aware landing states that prioritize the metrics most relevant to each user's responsibilities.

Process
  • Discovery: interviewed 15+ stakeholders across medical affairs, commercial, operations, and regional PSP managers
  • Mapped the end-to-end patient journey across 6 therapeutic areas to identify common and divergent data needs
  • Designed 4 dashboard layouts: executive overview, program deep-dive, patient cohort view, and geographic analysis
  • Ran 3 rounds of usability testing with healthcare professionals and program managers
  • Iterated on data visualization patterns — tested bar charts, funnels, cohort timelines, and geographic heatmaps to find the most effective representation for each metric
  • Worked with data engineering to define real-time data pipelines and refresh frequencies
Outcome & Impact
Efficient
Operational efficiency — replaced manual reporting with real-time interactive dashboards
↑
Improved patient engagement through early identification of at-risk patients
Faster
Decision-making — from days of data assembly to real-time insights
↑
Enhanced treatment adherence through proactive intervention signals
Reflection

This was my first deep engagement with healthcare data visualization, and the biggest learning was how different the stakes are. In finance, a delayed report costs money. In healthcare, a delayed intervention can affect a patient's treatment outcome. That weight shaped every design decision — from the urgency of early-warning indicators to the clarity of geographic distribution maps.

The patient journey framing was the breakthrough. Once we stopped organizing data by system and started organizing it by patient experience, every stakeholder — medical, commercial, operations — could finally see themselves in the same dashboard.

Forge

Redesigning the developer experience, documentation architecture, and knowledge hub for Atlassian's cloud-native app development platform — enabling faster adoption, clearer onboarding, and cross-team alignment.

Role Senior Product Designer
Duration 12 months
Domain Developer Experience · Platform UX
Type Knowledge Architecture · Workflow Optimization
On this page
Context

Forge is Atlassian's cloud-native platform for building and hosting apps directly on Atlassian products. It enables developers to create custom integrations, automate workflows, and extend Jira, Confluence, and other products without managing their own infrastructure. But despite its technical power, the platform struggled with adoption. Internal teams had low awareness, documentation was scattered across GitHub, Bitbucket, and internal wikis, and there was no unified onboarding path for new developers.

Problem

The platform faced multiple adoption barriers simultaneously. Internal teams didn't know what Forge could do for them — awareness was low despite significant engineering investment. Documentation lived in fragmented locations with no single source of truth. Teams were independently building duplicate, inconsistent solutions because they couldn't discover what already existed. New developers had no clear learning path, and the platform's perceived complexity was amplified by outdated or unclear documentation. There was also no visibility into the platform's feature roadmap, making it hard for teams to plan around Forge capabilities.

Strategy & Approach

The strategy was to transform Forge from a technically capable but poorly understood platform into one that developers actually want to use. This meant fixing the experience layer, not just the code:

  • Conducted research with internal developers across 8+ teams to map their journey from discovery to deployment — identifying every friction point
  • Designed a unified knowledge architecture that consolidates documentation from GitHub, Bitbucket, and internal docs into a single, searchable hub
  • Created a structured onboarding flow — from "What is Forge?" to "Deploy your first app" — with progressive complexity
  • Built a component library showcase with real use cases to demonstrate platform capabilities and reduce the "I didn't know it could do that" problem
  • Designed the information architecture for the documentation site with clear navigation, versioning, and contextual cross-linking
  • Supported the open-source release strategy by ensuring documentation met public-facing credibility standards
Key Design Decisions

Developer journey, not doc pages. Traditional documentation is organized by feature. We reorganized it around developer tasks — "Build an app," "Add authentication," "Deploy to production." Each journey is self-contained with the context needed to complete the task.

Show, don't tell. The biggest trust barrier was "Does this actually work?" We designed a showcase of real use cases — internal apps already built on Forge — with architecture diagrams, code snippets, and impact metrics. This turned skeptics into adopters.

Single source, multiple entry points. Developers find docs through search, direct links, IDE integrations, and Slack references. We designed the knowledge hub to work as a single source of truth that's accessible from all these entry points without duplication.

Progressive disclosure for complexity. Forge is a powerful platform with deep APIs. Instead of exposing everything upfront, we layered information — quick start for beginners, API reference for intermediate users, architecture patterns for advanced use cases.

Roadmap as a trust signal. We surfaced the platform roadmap directly in the developer hub. Teams could see what's coming, plan their integrations, and contribute feedback — turning the platform from a black box into a collaborative tool.

Process
  • Discovery: interviewed developers from 8+ internal teams, shadowed onboarding sessions, and analyzed support tickets to map pain points
  • Audited all existing documentation across 5 platforms — identified 40% redundancy and 30% outdated content
  • Designed the information architecture — tested 3 navigation models (feature-based, task-based, role-based) with developers; task-based won
  • Created the onboarding flow wireframes and tested with 10 new developers who had never used Forge
  • Built the use case showcase with 6 real internal apps, including architecture breakdowns and lessons learned
  • Iterated the knowledge hub design over 4 rounds based on developer feedback and analytics
  • Collaborated with technical writers, developer advocates, and engineering leads to ensure content accuracy and voice consistency
Outcome & Impact
↑
Increased platform adoption through clearer developer experience and onboarding
Aligned
Cross-team alignment — all contributing teams under one knowledge hub
↑
Improved developer productivity with structured documentation and clear learning paths
↓
Reduced engineering redundancy — teams discovering existing solutions instead of rebuilding
Reflection

This project taught me that developer experience is product design. Developers are users too — and they have the same frustrations with bad onboarding, unclear navigation, and hidden features as any end user. The difference is they'll abandon a tool faster because they can usually build an alternative.

The real use case showcase was the turning point. Before that, we were trying to convince teams with documentation. After, the apps spoke for themselves. Seeing a real team solve a real problem with Forge was worth more than 50 pages of API docs.

The 12-month duration gave us the rare luxury of measuring impact over time. We could see adoption curves respond to specific changes — the onboarding flow had the biggest single-week impact on new developer activation.

Criterion

Reimagining Atlassian's internal performance regression detection tool — from a single-product prototype to a scalable, unified platform serving all engineering teams.

Role Senior Product Designer
Duration 5 months
Domain Platform UX · Developer Tools
Type Performance Monitoring · Regression Detection
On this page
Context

Criterion started as an internal tool built by two developers to detect performance regressions in a single Atlassian product. Performance regressions — where system performance degrades after code or configuration changes — are one of the most costly issues in software delivery. Catching them early means shipping confidently. Catching them late means degraded user experience and expensive rollbacks. When leadership saw the tool's potential, it was expanded across all teams. But it wasn't designed for that scale, and the cracks were showing.

Problem

Criterion was built to solve one team's problem — and it worked well for that. But scaling it across Atlassian revealed fundamental issues. The tool wasn't designed for multi-team, multi-product use. Workflows were complex and manual, requiring time-consuming investigations that dragged down productivity. Engineers had to switch between Criterion, Lighthouse, and other tools to piece together a full picture of performance regressions. Insights were scattered, visibility was poor, and there was no alignment with Atlassian's core application workflows — it felt like a foreign tool bolted onto the development process.

Strategy & Approach

The goal was to transform Criterion from a single-product prototype into a unified, scalable regression-detection platform that fits naturally into how Atlassian engineering teams already work:

  • Interviewed engineers across 6 product teams to understand how they currently detect, investigate, and resolve performance regressions
  • Mapped the end-to-end regression investigation workflow — from alert to resolution — identifying where time was being lost
  • Designed a unified interface that consolidates Criterion, Lighthouse, and other monitoring signals into one view
  • Created an integration strategy with Jira and Bitbucket so regression alerts appear directly in engineering workflows
  • Standardized the regression detection experience across all teams while allowing product-specific thresholds and configurations
Key Design Decisions

One pane of glass. Engineers were context-switching between 3–4 tools to investigate a single regression. We designed a single investigation view that pulls in all relevant data — performance metrics, code changes, deployment history, and historical baselines — so engineers never have to leave Criterion.

Meaningful alerts, not noise. The old system generated too many alerts with too little context. We redesigned the alerting system to include severity scoring, probable cause attribution, and affected user impact estimates — so engineers know instantly whether an alert needs immediate attention or can wait.

Integrated, not bolted on. Criterion needed to feel like part of Atlassian's development workflow, not an external tool. We designed deep integrations with Jira (regression tickets auto-created with context) and Bitbucket (regression indicators visible in PR reviews), so the tool meets engineers where they already work.

Scalable by design. The original architecture assumed a single product. We redesigned the IA to support product-level, team-level, and org-level views — with configurable thresholds per product so each team can define what "regression" means for their context.

Process
  • Discovery: shadowed regression investigation sessions with 6 engineering teams to map real workflows and pain points
  • Audited the existing Criterion interface — identified 12 usability issues and 5 critical gaps in the investigation flow
  • Designed 3 investigation workflow prototypes: alert-driven, change-driven, and timeline-driven — tested with 8 engineers
  • Alert-driven won — engineers start from an alert and need to quickly determine severity, cause, and action
  • Designed the Jira and Bitbucket integration flows — validated with engineering managers and DevOps leads
  • Delivered the redesign in 2 phases: Phase 1 (unified view + alerting) and Phase 2 (integrations + multi-product support)
Outcome & Impact
↑
Higher productivity — reduced time spent on manual regression investigations
Unified
Single interface replacing fragmented multi-tool investigation workflows
Standard
Cross-team consistency in regression detection and response processes
Earlier
Regressions caught before production — reducing user-facing performance issues
Reflection

Criterion taught me what happens when a tool is built for one context and forced to serve many. The original design wasn't bad — it just wasn't designed for scale. The lesson is that scalability isn't just a technical concern; it's a UX concern. An interface that works for one team can become unusable when 20 teams have different mental models.

The Jira integration turned out to be the highest-impact design decision. Engineers were already in Jira all day — surfacing regression data directly in their existing tickets removed the biggest adoption barrier. Sometimes the best design decision is meeting users where they already are instead of asking them to come to you.

Hackathon Portal

An internal platform that takes employees from hackathon discovery to project submission — enabling team registration, collaboration, and giving leadership full visibility into participation and challenge traction.

Role UX Designer
Duration 1 month
Domain Event Platform · Internal Tools
Type Registration Flow · Submission Management
On this page
Context

EPAM runs internal hackathons to drive innovation, but the process was held together with spreadsheets, email threads, and manual coordination. There was no centralized platform where employees could browse problem statements, form teams, and submit their work. Organizers had limited visibility into participation, and the entire experience felt disconnected — from discovering a challenge to submitting a final project.

Problem

The hackathon experience was fragmented at every stage. Problem statements weren't collected in one place — employees had to hunt through emails, Slack channels, and internal wikis to find challenges. Registration was manual and inefficient, with no structured way to form or manage teams. There was no system for project submission or tracking, making it difficult for organizers to manage entries or evaluate results. Participation across departments was hard to measure, and there was poor visibility into which problem statements were gaining traction — valuable signal that was simply being lost.

Strategy & Approach

Given the tight 1-month timeline, the approach was focused and ruthless about scope. The goal was to ship a complete end-to-end experience that handles the full hackathon lifecycle:

  • Mapped the hackathon journey from an employee's perspective — from hearing about it to submitting a project — and identified the 5 critical moments that needed to feel effortless
  • Designed a discovery interface with clear categorization of problem statements, making it easy to browse, filter, and understand each challenge
  • Created a streamlined team registration flow — join an existing team or create a new one, with role assignments and team size constraints
  • Built a project submission workflow with structured fields, file uploads, and deadline visibility
  • Designed an organizer dashboard for tracking participation by department, challenge popularity, and submission status
Key Design Decisions

Browse-first, not register-first. Most internal platforms force registration before you can see anything. We let employees browse all problem statements without logging in. Friction comes after interest, not before.

One-click team formation. Team registration was the biggest pain point in previous hackathons. We designed a flow where you can create a team, invite colleagues via a shareable link, and lock in your challenge — all in under 2 minutes.

Live traction signals. We surfaced how many teams had selected each problem statement. This served dual purposes — helping employees find popular challenges and helping organizers see engagement patterns in real time.

Submission as a structured artifact. Instead of accepting freeform uploads, we designed a structured submission format — problem addressed, approach, demo link, tech stack, and team reflections. This made evaluation consistent and fair across all entries.

Process
  • Kickoff: interviewed 3 hackathon organizers and 8 past participants to understand what worked and what didn't in previous events
  • Mapped the end-to-end journey and prioritized the 5 critical flows: discovery, registration, team formation, submission, and organizer tracking
  • Designed wireframes for all 5 flows in the first week — validated with organizers and 5 employees
  • Moved to high-fidelity designs in week 2, incorporating feedback and edge cases (late registration, team changes, deadline extensions)
  • Delivered final designs and interaction specs in week 3, with a handoff document covering all states and error conditions
  • Supported engineering during week 4 implementation with rapid design QA and iteration
Outcome & Impact
↑
Higher participation — lower friction drove more teams to register and submit
Smooth
End-to-end experience from discovery to submission in one platform
Visible
Centralized visibility into department participation and challenge traction
Efficient
Structured submissions made evaluation consistent and faster for organizers
Reflection

One month. That's all we had. It was the tightest timeline I've worked with, and it forced a clarity I don't always get on longer projects. There was no room for feature creep or second-guessing — every design decision had to be justified by its impact on the hackathon experience.

The browse-first decision was the most impactful. In past hackathons, employees heard about it too late or didn't bother because finding relevant challenges required too much effort. Making problem statements browsable and categorized — before asking anyone to register — changed the conversion funnel entirely.

SkillUp — Paid Certificate Funnel

Creating the first monetization layer for Simplilearn's free learning platform — turning 3 years of zero revenue into 1,000+ orders in 5 weeks through low-cost certificates and value-added services.

RoleUX Designer
Duration7 weeks
DomainProduct Growth · Monetization
TypeConversion Funnel · Revenue UX
On this page
Context

SkillUp is Simplilearn's free learning platform offering 120+ short, high-quality courses. It had massive traction — growing monthly active users, strong engagement — but generated zero direct revenue for 3 years. The business couldn't justify scaling the platform without a revenue model, but monetization had to be handled carefully to avoid alienating the free user base that made SkillUp valuable in the first place.

Problem

The core tension: high engagement, zero revenue. Users had no way to validate or showcase their learning — they completed courses but had nothing to show for it. There was no conversion path from free learners to paying customers, no premium add-ons to upsell, and missing incentives for course completion. The business was stuck — unable to scale SkillUp without revenue, unable to monetize without risking user trust.

Strategy & Approach

The approach was to add value before extracting revenue — giving learners something they actually want, not just a paywall:

  • Researched what free learners valued most — certificates for LinkedIn/resumes ranked #1 across all segments
  • Designed a low-cost certificate offering ($5–15) that feels worth paying for — credible design, verifiable link, shareable on LinkedIn
  • Created a conversion funnel that surfaces at the right moment — after course completion, when the learner is most receptive
  • Added complementary upsells: mentorship sessions, CV-writing help, and career guidance as optional add-ons
  • Designed the funnel for both mobile and web, ensuring a seamless purchase experience on either platform
Key Design Decisions

Post-completion trigger, not mid-course. We tested placing the certificate offer at different journey points. Post-completion won — learners feel accomplished and are psychologically primed to invest in validation. Mid-course placement felt pushy and hurt completion rates.

Show the certificate before the paywall. We designed a preview of the personalized certificate — name, course, date, verification code — before asking for payment. Seeing the artifact made the value tangible and increased conversion significantly.

Bundled value, not just a PDF. A certificate alone felt thin. Bundling it with a LinkedIn share flow, a downloadable badge, and optional add-ons made the offering feel like a package worth paying for.

Reflection

CropScope is the project I'm most proud of from a human-impact perspective. Designing for farmers in rural India forced me to rethink every assumption I had about digital products. The user isn't sitting at a desk — they're standing in a field. They might be using a smartphone for the first time. Their primary language isn't English. Connectivity is spotty at best.

The camera-first insight came directly from field research. When I watched a farmer try to describe a leaf discoloration in words, I realized the scan interaction had to be the core of the product — not a feature buried in a menu. The most impactful design decisions come from watching real people struggle with real problems in their real environment.
