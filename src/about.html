About Me

I'm a Product & UX Designer who has worked across multiple domains, building and evolving products that balance user needs, business goals, and technical constraints.

I'm generally known for taking a pragmatic, research-informed approach to design — one that's comfortable with ambiguity, honest about trade-offs, and focused on outcomes that actually matter to the business. I've shipped products that scaled, and I've shipped products that failed. Both taught me something.

8+ years in product & UX · Healthcare · Pharma · SaaS · Fintech · Open to opportunities

My Background

I didn't start in design. My early career was in a different field entirely, and the transition into product and UX happened gradually — driven more by curiosity than a master plan. I found myself drawn to problems that sat at the intersection of people, systems, and business logic. Design turned out to be the discipline that let me work on all three at once.

Over the years, I moved through startups, mid-stage companies, and enterprise environments. Each taught me something different: startups taught me speed and resourcefulness; enterprise taught me navigating complexity, stakeholders, and organizational inertia. I've worked in healthcare, pharma, SaaS, fintech, and consumer — and every domain shift forced me to rebuild my assumptions from scratch.

The biggest shift in my thinking came from working on products that failed. Shipping something, watching it underperform, and having to diagnose why — that experience changed how I approach design more than any success story. It made me less attached to solutions and more invested in understanding the problem.

How I Think About Design

Principles that guide my work — not rules, but defaults I return to.

Design is problem framing, not just execution

The most impactful work I've done started before any wireframe. Understanding what problem we're actually solving — and whether it's the right problem — matters more than how polished the output is. I spend a disproportionate amount of time in problem definition because it saves time everywhere else.

Trade-offs are the real design decisions

There's rarely a solution that optimizes for user experience, business impact, and technical feasibility equally. I focus on making those trade-offs visible and intentional — not pretending they don't exist. A good designer makes the best decision given real constraints, not the ideal decision in a vacuum.

Comfort with ambiguity and incomplete information

Most real projects don't start with a clear brief. I'm comfortable working with partial data, conflicting stakeholder inputs, and shifting requirements. I'd rather start with uncertainty and iterate toward clarity than wait for perfect conditions that never arrive.

Willingness to challenge requirements

I've recommended not building features, rolling back shipped work, and questioning assumptions that the team had already committed to. It's not comfortable, but it's often the most valuable thing a designer can do. The goal is the right outcome, not consensus for its own sake.

Failures are data, not embarrassments

I've been part of products that didn't work. I include those in my portfolio because they're part of the record. What matters isn't a perfect track record — it's whether you can diagnose what went wrong, extract learning, and apply it next time.

Beyond Work

Things that shape how I think, even when I'm not designing.

Reading

I read a lot about systems thinking, behavioral economics, and organizational decision-making. Books like Thinking in Systems and Thinking, Fast and Slow have influenced my design approach more than most design books.

Writing

I occasionally write about design decisions, product trade-offs, and lessons from projects that didn't go as planned. Writing helps me clarify my own thinking.

Side Projects

I tinker with small tools and prototypes — mostly to explore ideas outside of client constraints. It keeps me close to craft and gives me space to experiment without stakeholders.

Mentoring

I mentor early-career designers informally. Teaching forces you to articulate what you actually believe about design — and occasionally realize you were wrong.

How I Work

For anyone wondering what it's like to collaborate with me.

Bias toward clarity

I prefer explicit communication over assumptions. If something is ambiguous, I'll name it rather than gloss over it.

Respect for constraints

I don't treat engineering limitations or business timelines as obstacles. They're design inputs. The best work happens within real boundaries.

Collaborative but opinionated

I bring a point of view to every discussion, but I hold it loosely. I'm interested in the best outcome, not being right.

Comfortable saying "I don't know"

I'd rather admit uncertainty and figure it out than pretend I have an answer. Teams that normalize this tend to make better decisions.

Focus on outcomes over output

I measure success by what shipped and what it achieved — not by how many screens or deliverables were produced.

Low ego, high ownership

I'll do the unglamorous work if it moves the project forward. I care more about the product being good than about who gets credit.
